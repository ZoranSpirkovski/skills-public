# archive-3-2-questions-schools-should-be-asking

## Summary

Lesson 3.2 reframes AI in education as an intellectual-property problem. Teachers produce lesson plans, methods, and curricula worth hundreds of thousands of dollars and receive none of the licensing value. Professors generate research that earns institutions millions. Students surrender code and creative work to the university. Van Clief proposes three shifts: portable AI ownership for students, IP rights for teachers, and revenue sharing when institutional knowledge generates value. The most radical proposal is that every student should graduate owning an AI trained on four years of their intellectual development, a deployable asset that companies can license.

## Key claims

1. Teachers and professors produce institutional IP worth enormous sums and do not share in its downstream value.
2. CS majors build tools in class that the university, not the student, owns. This mirrors forcing art students to surrender their paintings.
3. Students should be given a personal AI agent on day one of university and train it across four years of their own work.
4. The graduating student should own both the AI and the data behind it, entirely and privately. Without that ownership, the model fails.
5. Companies could license a graduate's personalized AI instead of hiring the graduate, paying for trained judgment that works 24/7.
6. The university becomes a pipeline for both human judgment and technical capability, taking a small licensing cut to fund better education.
7. The right questions for schools are: who owns the AI trained on student work, how do we help teachers monetize innovations, what if degrees came with deployable AI assets, how do we share revenue from institutional knowledge, why should students not graduate with AI companies.
8. GitHub showed students could own their code. Bandcamp did it for musicians. Substack did it for writers. Education is the last holdout where IP flows only upward.
9. "The university that figures this out first wins." "Graduate with your own AI company" is a stronger pitch than any ranking.
10. Lessons 2.1 through 2.3 argue AI makes human expertise more valuable. This lesson asks to whom that value flows; the answer today is institutions, not individuals.
