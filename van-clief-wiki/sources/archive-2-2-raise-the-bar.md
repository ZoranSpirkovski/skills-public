# archive-2-2-raise-the-bar

## Summary

Lesson 2.2 pairs a December 2023 video with a 2026 written companion. The argument is blunt: if students can cheat with AI on your assignments, your assignments are too easy. Each major classroom technology raised the baseline before. Calculators did not destroy math. The internet did not destroy research. AI does the same thing to writing and analysis. The companion reframes the coding pyramid (machine code, C, Python, LLMs) as the seed of computational orchestration and the 60/30/10 framework. "Raise the bar" was the right diagnosis; the frameworks that came later (folder architecture, Agent Charter, 60/30/10) are the prescription.

## Key claims

1. If a student can pass an AI-written submission through your assignment, the assignment tests recall and generic synthesis, not judgment.
2. Classroom technology stacks in a pattern: calculator raised the math baseline, the internet raised the research baseline, AI raises the writing and analysis baseline.
3. Each stack step moves the old expert skill down the curriculum and pushes senior-level work higher.
4. A linguistics professor's advanced grammar work should become the freshman baseline once AI handles basic grammar; her top-level work pushes higher still.
5. The coding pyramid (machine code, C, Python, LLMs) shows each abstraction layer lets a novice do what was expert work. LLMs are the next language layer, now driven in English.
6. The pyramid is the seed of computational orchestration. The 60/30/10 framework (60% traditional code, 30% rule-based logic, 10% AI) is what grew from it.
7. "Generic writing is now visible." Frequent AI use trains the eye to recognize lazy AI output better than AI detectors do.
8. An assignment that generates three AI essays and asks students to grade and rewrite them teaches editing, critical reading, tone, and structure simultaneously, and sidesteps the cheating debate.
9. "Raise the bar" as a diagnosis needed a method underneath. The folder architecture, Agent Charter, and 60/30/10 framework fill that gap.
10. Raising the bar without raising the support produces frustration, not growth. Training exercises that generate real workflow data fix this.
