# archive-2-3-more-human

## Summary

Lesson 2.3 pairs a December 2023 video with a 2026 written companion. The video opens with a personal story: 2019, a Mount Fuji typhoon, Van Clief and a friend find an injured hiker off-trail and drag him up the mountain to a shrine. The story frames the thesis. AI threatens the robotic, the bureaucratic, the formulaic. It does not threaten the human parts of our work. It clears space for them. The second half reads a Wall Street Journal response article on AI in academic writing. The companion calls this the earliest public version of an argument that still holds: AI acts as a mirror that exposes where our systems were already mechanical.

## Key claims

1. AI removes the robotic, not the human. The tasks AI handles well were already formulaic before AI arrived.
2. The experiences that make a person irreplaceable are not skills but the specific chain of decisions that put them where no one else is.
3. "AI is highlighting what is and isn't human in us." When a language model can pass a PhD review, the system's rigor is what gets exposed, not the model.
4. The scribe-and-printing-press parallel: the press handled scale, the scribes handled meaning. Scribes did not lose value; their work specialized. The same pattern holds for AI.
5. "The value lies in the human intellect's ability to question, probe, and guide." Later frameworks (folder architecture, Model Workspace Protocol, five-layer context hierarchy) implement this principle.
6. Creating a high-quality AI output requires deep domain understanding. The prompt and the probing are the intellectual work.
7. "Back in my day syndrome" blocks engagement with AI. The 2026 companion walks this claim back slightly to give more weight to the real fear senior experts carry.
8. The Mount Fuji story functions as an existence proof: a single moment of judgment, empathy, and presence that cannot be replicated by any model.
9. The PhD thought experiment, calculator analogy, and printing-press parallel overlap with Lessons 2.1 and 1.2. This lesson's unique contribution is the Fuji story and the "more human" philosophical argument.
10. The "more human" thesis now extends beyond education into Van Clief's Ethics Engine research and his governance treatise on AI consciousness and digital sovereignty.
