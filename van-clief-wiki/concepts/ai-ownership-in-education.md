# ai-ownership-in-education

Van Clief's structural argument that the real AI-in-education question is not pedagogy but intellectual property. Today teachers build methods worth hundreds of thousands of dollars and see none of the licensing value. Students surrender code and creative work to universities. Van Clief proposes three shifts: portable AI ownership (every student trains a personal AI across four years and graduates owning it), teacher IP rights (educators own their methods and license them), and revenue sharing on institutional knowledge. The strongest prescription: a graduating student should leave with a deployable AI asset trained on their own thinking, ownable and privately held, that companies can license instead of hiring.

## Claims

- Teachers produce institutional IP worth enormous sums and share none of the downstream value [sources/archive-3-2-questions-schools-should-be-asking]
- CS majors build tools in class that the university owns; this mirrors taking art students' paintings [sources/archive-3-2-questions-schools-should-be-asking]
- Every student should be given a personal AI on day one and train it across four years of coursework [sources/archive-3-2-questions-schools-should-be-asking]
- The graduating student must own the AI and the data behind it entirely and privately for the model to work [sources/archive-3-2-questions-schools-should-be-asking]
- Companies could license a graduate's personalized AI instead of hiring the graduate: 24/7 trained judgment [sources/archive-3-2-questions-schools-should-be-asking]
- The right questions for schools include who owns the AI trained on student work, how to monetize teacher innovations, what if degrees came with deployable AI assets [sources/archive-3-2-questions-schools-should-be-asking]
- GitHub, Bandcamp, and Substack proved IP can flow to creators; education is the last industry where it still flows only upward [sources/archive-3-2-questions-schools-should-be-asking]
- "Graduate with your own AI company" is a stronger university pitch than any ranking [sources/archive-3-2-questions-schools-should-be-asking]
