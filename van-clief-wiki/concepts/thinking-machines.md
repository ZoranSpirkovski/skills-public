# thinking-machines

Van Clief's umbrella term in Archive 1.2 for the 2,000-year chain of programmable mechanisms and computational devices - from Heron's theatre through Al-Jazari, Jacquard, Babbage, Lovelace, and Turing to modern AI. The term is meant to collapse the artificial boundary between "historical automata" and "modern AI", showing them as successive layers of the same abstraction project.

## Claims

- Every generation builds thinking machines, normalizes them, and forgets the complexity underneath [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- The pattern impossible → normalized → forgotten → next impossible is the through-line of computing history [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- The question is not "can machines think?" but "what patterns will we abstract next?" - a reframing attributed to the philosophical tradition Van Clief revisits [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- The Abstraction series in Foundation Module 2 revisits the same sequence (Jacquard 1804, Lovelace and Babbage, the Pentium era, cosmic-ray bit flips) to argue AI is the newest layer and we are in its "moth in the relay" phase [sources/foundation-2-2-one-line-of-python]
- The Archive Lesson 2.1 workshop extends the history back to Aristotle (autonomous-tool thought experiment in The Politics) and forward through Roger Bacon's Opus Majus [sources/archive-2-1-mindset-before-method]
- The Descartes-Lovelace-Turing sequence frames the Turing-Test-replacement question in Lesson 3.1: machine thought, origination, behavior, and now recognition between specific configurations [sources/archive-3-1-beyond-the-turing-test]
