# ai-removes-the-robotic

Van Clief's philosophical thesis from Archive Lesson 2.3. AI does not threaten the human parts of our work. It threatens the robotic, the bureaucratic, the formulaic parts that were already mechanical before AI arrived. AI acts as a mirror: when a model passes a task, that task was mechanical. When a model cannot pass a task, that task holds judgment, experience, or presence that no current system can replicate. The Mount Fuji story (saving a hiker during a typhoon) is the existence proof. Such moments are not skills. They are the specific chain of decisions that put a person where no one else is.

## Claims

- AI eats the mechanical work and leaves the human work standing [sources/archive-2-3-more-human]
- What makes a person irreplaceable is not skill but the chain of decisions that put them in a unique position [sources/archive-2-3-more-human]
- "AI is highlighting what is and isn't human in us." AI exposes where systems were already robotic [sources/archive-2-3-more-human]
- If a language model can pass your PhD review process, the revealed problem is the review process, not the model [sources/archive-2-3-more-human]
- The scribe-and-printing-press parallel: the press handled scale, scribes handled meaning; both survived because the value split along that axis [sources/archive-2-3-more-human]
- "The value lies in the human intellect's ability to question, probe, and guide" (Van Clief's WSJ response article) [sources/archive-2-3-more-human]
- The Mount Fuji typhoon rescue is the story Van Clief uses to ground the thesis: a specific, unrepeatable moment that cannot be automated [sources/archive-2-3-more-human]
