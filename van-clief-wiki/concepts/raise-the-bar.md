# raise-the-bar

Van Clief's diagnosis of the AI-in-education cheating panic. If a student can pass your assignment with a chat prompt, the assignment was testing recall or generic synthesis, both now baseline. The technology-stacking pattern holds: calculators raised the math baseline, the internet raised the research baseline, AI raises the writing and analysis baseline. Each prior cycle moved the old expert skill down the curriculum and pushed senior work higher. The raise-the-bar move also applies to business. If AI is eating part of your industry's output, the right response is not to ban it. It is to raise the baseline and train above it.

## Claims

- "If students can cheat with AI on your assignments, your assignments are too easy" [sources/archive-2-2-raise-the-bar]
- Technology stacks in education: calculator, internet, AI, each raising the baseline of what is expected [sources/archive-2-2-raise-the-bar]
- Each stacking step moves the old expert skill down the curriculum and pushes senior work higher [sources/archive-2-2-raise-the-bar]
- Advanced linguistics work (grammar structure) should become the freshman baseline once AI handles basic grammar; the professor's top work pushes further up [sources/archive-2-2-raise-the-bar]
- An assignment that generates three AI essays and asks students to grade and rewrite them teaches editing, critical reading, tone, and structure at once, while sidestepping the cheating debate [sources/archive-2-2-raise-the-bar]
- "Raise the bar" is a correct diagnosis but needs a method underneath: the folder architecture, Agent Charter, and 60/30/10 framework are the later prescription [sources/archive-2-2-raise-the-bar]
- Raising the bar without raising the support produces frustration, not growth [sources/archive-2-2-raise-the-bar]
- The calculator-in-classroom analogy: teachers learned when to allow it and when to restrict it, rather than banning [sources/archive-2-1-mindset-before-method] [sources/archive-2-2-raise-the-bar]
