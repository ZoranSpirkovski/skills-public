# joseph-marie-jacquard

French weaver and inventor whose punched-card loom, patented 23 December 1801, made the weaving pattern itself into a program - chains of punched pasteboard cards selected which threads the loom raised. Van Clief cites Jacquard as the hinge where the concept of "stored instructions" first escaped dedicated automata and became general.

## Claims

- Patented the punched-card loom 23 December 1801 [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- Each card controlled one row of the pattern; holes passed pins to raise threads, no hole blocked pins - the card was the program [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- By 1812 France had 11,000 Jacquard looms; complex patterns that once took months now took days [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- Sparked the first recorded technology backlash: Lyon weavers rioted, attacked Jacquard, and burned looms in fear of unemployment [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- Van Clief's "one line of Python" lesson places Jacquard as the origin of binary control logic: hole or no hole, the first digital primitive [sources/foundation-2-2-one-line-of-python]
- In 1839 the loom wove a silk portrait of Jacquard himself requiring 24,000 cards, each with over a thousand hole positions [sources/foundation-2-2-one-line-of-python]
