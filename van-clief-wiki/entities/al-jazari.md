# al-jazari

Chief engineer to the 12th–13th century Artuqid dynasty. Completed a manuscript in 1206 (the year he died) describing fifty mechanical devices, including an eleven-foot castle clock that could be reprogrammed daily and a four-figure musical automaton band programmable by repositioning pegs on cylinders. Van Clief positions him as the inventor of the mechanical music sequencer and the first systematic documenter of camshafts.

## Claims

- 1206 manuscript describes 50 mechanical devices, including the reprogrammable castle clock [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- Musical automaton band: four robot musicians whose output changed when users moved pegs on rotating cylinders - a mechanical music sequencer [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- First systematic documentation of camshafts (rotary-to-linear motion conversion), five centuries before the Industrial Revolution relied on them [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- Science Museum London reconstructed his scribe clock from his instructions in 1976 - it worked perfectly [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- Donald Hill (translator): "impossible to over-emphasize the importance of Al-Jazari's work in the history of engineering" - yet most people have never heard his name [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- Cited in the Flagler workshop as a 13th-century engineer whose Book of Knowledge of Mechanical Devices describes machines that perform human tasks [sources/archive-2-1-mindset-before-method]

## Relationships
