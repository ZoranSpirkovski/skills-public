# rene-descartes

French philosopher, 17th century. In *Discourse on the Method* (1637) proposed two tests for distinguishing a human from a machine, ~300 years before Turing proposed his. Van Clief argues AI has now passed the first (contextual language use) but that the second (acting from knowledge rather than the disposition of organs) is still unresolved - and perhaps unanswerable, because humans may be mechanical in ways too intricate to recognize.

## Claims

- 1637 *Discourse on the Method* outlined two tests of machine-vs-human distinction, predating the Turing Test by ~313 years [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- First test: a machine could never "arrange [words] variously so as appositely to reply to what is said in its presence" - modern LLMs have broken this [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- Second test: machines would "inevitably fail in certain others from which it could be discovered that they did not act from knowledge, but solely from the disposition of their organs" - Van Clief suggests humans may fail this test too [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- Cited in the Flagler workshop as the early modern author who took seriously what a thinking machine would need in order to be distinguished from a human [sources/archive-2-1-mindset-before-method]
- In Lesson 3.1 Van Clief rewrites Descartes' "I think, therefore I am" as "You think, therefore I am", arguing consciousness may require an observer [sources/archive-3-1-beyond-the-turing-test]
