# alan-turing

English mathematician and computer scientist, 20th century. His 1950 paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" proposed the imitation game (Turing Test) and systematically demolished nine objections to machine intelligence - including Lovelace's Objection (objection #6). Turing's move: rather than program adult intelligence directly, build a "child machine" and teach it, so its eventual behavior is something we enable but do not directly program.

## Claims

- 1950 paper demolished nine objections to machine intelligence, including [[ada-lovelace]]'s Objection [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- Proposed teaching "child machines" rather than programming adult intelligence: origination through education, not explicit instruction [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- 1947: "If a machine is expected to be infallible, it cannot also be intelligent" - the quest for determinism is the enemy of intelligence [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- Argued machines would need to guess, be wrong, work probabilistically - "just like humans" [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- Cited in the Flagler workshop as a forerunner of AI who rebutted Descartes and Lovelace on machine thought [sources/archive-2-1-mindset-before-method]
- Van Clief argues the Turing Test was quietly cleared by March 2026 across conversation and video; the next test is recognition between thinking things, not between human and machine [sources/archive-3-1-beyond-the-turing-test]
