# ada-lovelace

English mathematician, 19th century. In 1843 translated Luigi Menabrea's article on the Analytical Engine and added notes three times longer than the original. Note G contained the first recognized computer algorithm (calculation of Bernoulli numbers), demonstrating loops, conditional branching, and storage of intermediate results. Also authored what became known as "Lady Lovelace's Objection" - that machines cannot originate - which held as a definitive view for a century until Turing refuted it.

## Claims

- Note G (1843) is the first computer algorithm: loops, conditional branching, intermediate storage, computing Bernoulli numbers [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- Observed the [[joseph-marie-jacquard]] loom / [[charles-babbage]] Analytical Engine structural parallel [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- "The Analytical Engine has no pretensions whatever to originate anything. It can do whatever we know how to order it to perform." - Lovelace's Objection [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- Objection held for a century as the definitive view that machines cannot create or think [sources/archive-1-2-2000-year-overnight-success]
- "The Analytical Engine weaves algebraic patterns just as the Jacquard loom weaves flowers and leaves" - the original statement of computing as structured pattern execution [sources/foundation-2-2-one-line-of-python]
- Cited in the Flagler workshop as a brilliant mathematician who denied machines could think but believed they could create music or art [sources/archive-2-1-mindset-before-method]
- Sits at the 1843 node of the Descartes-Lovelace-Turing sequence Van Clief uses to frame the Turing-Test replacement question [sources/archive-3-1-beyond-the-turing-test]
